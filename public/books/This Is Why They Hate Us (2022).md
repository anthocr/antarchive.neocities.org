# This is Why They Hate Us (2022)
#novel #reading #queer

Author: Aaron H. Aceves 
### Rating
3.7/5
## Synopsis
Our main character Enrique "Quique" Luna is a troubled queer Latino that navigates through high school battling struggles that are synonymous with the queer experience. He has a crush on his best friend, Saleem Kanazi, who is initially oblivious to this crush. Over the course of the story he meets many friends and romantic interests that develop his character.

## Thoughts
Ounce I got about halfway through this book, I wasn't sure if I was going to enjoy it as much as the other queer stories I've read. Ounce the characters are introduced, the main character starts building romantic situation-ships (for lack of a better word) with all of the other male characters introduced in the story. This cycle grew to be a little repetitive for me and also just plain unrealistic. 

However, i think this book really does well in embodying the queer experience as a whole while also addressing specific struggles of bisexual men. The book comments on many stereotypes that bisexual people face, like having a preference, all bisexual people like men, etc. I find the depth of the main character refreshing and he feels realistic for the most part. The author depicts Quique as often lustful and the book does lean in to the 'bisexuals are hypersexual' stereotype a bit, but I believe that the author made to decision to have him interact with many men to develop his character. The main character is also 17 and is depicted as so.

A major part of this book focuses on mental health, especially the latter portion. Quique's mental health declines over the course of the story due to amount of just wild stuff that happens to him. I personally related a lot to his struggle of trying to accept yourself when you are up against external and internal obstacles. Finding value in yourself and life can be difficult when you don't feel accepted, leading many to isolate themselves and battle loneliness. 

This book does contain a few explicit scenes that I was not aware of beforehand which was a little shocking. However, I feel these scenes are not so incredibly explicit that they are unreadable or anything, but some may be uncomfortable listening to/ or reading these.

Overall, despite some flaws, this was a good read for me and the parts where I saw my younger self struggling the same as Quique impacted me deeply.
