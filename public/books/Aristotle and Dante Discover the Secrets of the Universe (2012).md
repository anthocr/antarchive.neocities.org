# Aristotle and Dante Discover the Secrets of the Universe (2012)
#reading #lgbt #novel #coming-of-age

Author: Benjamin Alire Sáenz
### Rating
5/5
## Synopsis
We follow our main character, Ari, through his early childhood to his later teen years. The book's narrative centers around Ari's friendship with Dante as well as other smaller relationships that Ari has. Ari struggles making friends and connecting with people, being a **social outcast** of sorts from an early age. This changes when Dante enters his life and their relationship guides Ari to become more '*scrutable*' as the book likes to say.

## Thoughts
This book is probably the best coming of age novel I've read in a very long time. The author excels at showcasing Ari's struggles through his mental framework and the circumstances that impact him. Both of the main characters are *Mexican American* and struggle with that identity. Ari's father is often reserved and emotionally withdrawn with him having a better but strained relationship with his mother. This contrasts well with Dante's parents who seem to be the prefect parents for their son, this is tested in the narrative as well.

One of the main themes of the book is the lack of guidance and nurture that he feels from his family, specifically his father. he feels incredibly alone and resorts to violence or substances to solve his problems due to this. "**Silent wars**" are a key theme mentioned in the book, with Ari's family coping with their past by burying it and silencing the topic. They exhibit this coping mechanism when it comes to Ari's brother, *Bernardo*, and also his father's time serving in Vietnam. This comes to a boiling point in Ari's later years, with his parents finally opening up about many of the details surrounding both events. This allows the family to heal and allows Ari to accept himself and also his relationship with Dante.

This novel is a **slow burn** one that I don't usually enjoy too much, but it made the story feel a lot more deep with the intimacy of Ari and Dante's relationship not being the main focus. This was refreshing for me since many novels with gay men are too centered around intimacy and the "*forbidden nature*" of it all. I would much rather see a deep bond of two characters built over the course of a novel then get a long drawn-out '*will they won't they*' trope that I'm frankly tired of seeing. Ari has to go through his entire journey and deal with the trauma and consequences of his actions to fully accept himself and Dante as lovers.

One issue I had with the book was simply that there wasn't more. I would've really enjoyed a small **epilogue** showing the two families uniting and supporting their children together, or exploring Dante and Ari's newfound relationship more. However, there is a sequel novel that fulfills this purpose I would assume.

Overall, I really enjoyed this novel and it was extremely refreshing for me. It's a well written story as well as showcasing a deep relationship that is easy to be invested in. Many of Ari's struggles are deeply personal to me and I can relate to his character so much, which definitely sank me deeper into the narrative. If you're looking for a good coming-of-age novel that has fantastic LGBTQ representation, I would strongly recommend this one.
